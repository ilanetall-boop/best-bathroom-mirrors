Things to Know Before You Buy

  • Size to your vanity, not your wall. A mirror should be a few inches narrower than the vanity below it. A 30-inch-tall mirror suits most single sinks; go 40 inches wide or more only for double vanities.
  • Color temperature matters more than raw brightness. Mirrors with adjustable white light let you switch between flattering warm light and the neutral daylight you need for makeup or shaving.
  • Anti-fog is a heating pad, not a coating. The built-in defoggers warm a zone of the glass so it never mists over, which is far more durable than spray-on coatings but adds to the price.
  • Check the mount and the outlet first. Backlit LED mirrors need a nearby 110V outlet or a hardwired connection, and most hang on a French cleat. Confirm you have power within a few feet before you buy.

A bathroom mirror is the one fixture you look at every single morning, and a bad one drags the whole room down. Dim overhead lighting throws shadows under your eyes, a fogged-up surface forces you to wipe it down mid-shave, and a flimsy frame can warp or spot within a year of humid use. An LED mirror solves the first two problems by building even, front-facing light into the glass itself.

The Competition

Plain frameless beveled wall mirrors. The unlit rectangular mirrors that fill big-box stores are cheaper, but they solve none of the lighting or fog problems that make a bathroom mirror frustrating. If you are reading a guide to LED mirrors, they are not what you want.
Hollywood vanity mirrors with exposed bulbs. The round-bulb makeup mirrors look great in photos, but the bulbs run hot, burn out one at a time, and throw less even light than the edge-to-edge LEDs in our picks. They are a styling choice, not a functional upgrade.
Smart mirrors with displays and voice assistants. Touchscreen mirrors that show the weather or stream video are appealing on paper, but they cost several times more than our picks, add software that can break, and do not light or de-fog any better. For almost everyone they are not worth it.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are LED bathroom mirrors hard to install?

Most hang on a French cleat (two screws into the wall) and plug into or hardwire to a nearby 110V outlet, so a confident DIYer can mount one in under an hour. The main requirement is power within a few feet of the mount. Larger mirrors like the 40-by-60-inch XRAMFY need two people and solid wall anchors because of their weight.

Do the anti-fog features on these mirrors actually work?

Yes, because they are heating pads rather than coatings. A low-wattage element behind the glass warms a zone of the mirror so condensation never forms there, and it lasts the life of the mirror, unlike spray-on anti-fog coatings, which wear off in a week or two. The heated zone is usually a section of the glass, not the entire surface.

What size LED mirror should I buy?

As a rule, your mirror should be a few inches narrower than the vanity beneath it. A 30-inch-tall mirror like our 36-by-30-inch top pick suits most single sinks; go 40 inches wide or more only for double vanities or large primary bathrooms. For a compact powder room, the 14-to-24-inch options here are a better fit.

LED mirror or a lit medicine cabinet: which should I get?

Choose a plain LED mirror like our top pick when you have storage elsewhere and want the largest possible reflective surface for the money. Choose a mirrored cabinet like the Snughome or recessed TokeShimi when counter clutter is the real problem and you are willing to trade some glass size for hidden shelves behind the mirror.
