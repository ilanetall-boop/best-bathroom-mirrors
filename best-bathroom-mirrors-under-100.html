Things to Know Before You Buy

  • Under $100 buys a lot: a clean frameless mirror, an LED model with built-in lighting, or a recessed medicine cabinet that doubles as storage.
  • Size matters more than brand. Measure your wall and vanity first. A 24-inch mirror over a 36-inch vanity looks undersized.
  • Frameless tempered-glass mirrors are the safest value. They resist humidity, won't rust at the edges, and suit nearly any decor.
  • LED and recessed features are useful, but they need an outlet or a cut into the wall. Check what your bathroom can accommodate before you commit.

How We Picked

We started with the mirrors people most often buy in this price range and narrowed the field on a few hard criteria. The glass had to be true tempered glass for safety and clarity, not the thin float glass that distorts your reflection at the edges. The design, framed or frameless, had to suit a wide range of bathrooms, which pushed us toward clean frameless styling and a few practical cabinets rather than anything trendy. And the mounting system had to be something a confident DIYer could handle in an afternoon without hiring a contractor.

We also made sure to include at least one mirror in each of the formats people actually search for: a basic frameless wall mirror, a larger statement mirror, an LED-lit model, a recessed medicine cabinet, and a couple of mid-size options for awkward walls. A good lineup under $100 should cover the small powder room and the shared family bath alike.

How We Tested

Because mirrors are hard to judge from a spec sheet, we focused on the things owners notice over months of daily use: whether the reflection stays distortion-free across the whole surface, how the edges and backing handle constant humidity, and how secure the mirror feels once it's hung. We weighed each pick's stated dimensions against the vanity sizes it's meant to sit above, and we flagged any model where buyers consistently reported fogging that wouldn't clear, rust spots, or flimsy hardware.

Where a mirror added features like LED lighting or hidden storage, we judged whether those extras actually earned their place or simply added points of failure and installation headaches. The goal was to find mirrors that still look and work as well a year in as they do on day one.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can you really get a good bathroom mirror under $100?

Yes. Our top pick costs $32.39 and uses the same tempered glass found in far pricier mirrors. Under $100 you can choose between a clean frameless mirror, a larger statement piece, an LED-lit model, or a recessed medicine cabinet. The main thing you give up at this price is high-end framing and smart extras like built-in defoggers.

What size bathroom mirror should I buy?

As a rule, your mirror should be roughly the width of your vanity or a few inches narrower, never wider. A 24-inch mirror suits a small single vanity, while a 36-inch model like our runner-up fits a standard or wide one. Measure your vanity and the open wall space above it before you order.

Are frameless mirrors safe and durable in a humid bathroom?

Frameless tempered-glass mirrors are a strong choice for humidity. Tempered glass is more impact-resistant than ordinary glass, and without a metal frame there is no trim to rust or trap moisture at the edges. The picks in this guide use tempered glass with aluminum backing for exactly that reason.

Do LED mirrors need an electrician to install?

It depends on your wall. If there is already an outlet or wiring where the mirror will hang, many LED mirrors plug in or hardwire easily. If not, you'll need to run power to that spot, which usually means hiring an electrician, a cost worth factoring in before you buy.
